[ Email deliverability ]

Get the login code into the inbox.

OTP codes land in spam when the sending domain isn't authenticated. Fix two things — route the email through Resend and prove you own the domain — and Gmail/Outlook will trust it.

Important: the login OTP is sent by Supabase Auth, not by your app's Resend code. Your EMAIL_FROM only controls notification emails (e.g. "shipped"). So the spam fix is: point Supabase Auth at Resend's SMTP (Step 1) and authenticate the domain (Steps 2–3). Steps 2–3 fix all your mail, OTP included.

Step 1

Send OTP through Resend, not Supabase's shared sender

Supabase's built-in email service uses a shared IP with poor reputation and is rate-limited — they explicitly say don't use it in production. Route auth email through your Resend account:

Supabase → Authentication → Emails → SMTP Settings → enable custom SMTP

  • Host  smtp.resend.com
  • Port  465 (or 587)
  • Username  resend
  • Password  your Resend API key
  • Sender email  noreply@mail.findersxkeepers.com (on the verified domain — never a gmail/free address)
  • Sender name  Finders × Keepers
Step 2

Authenticate the domain in Resend (SPF + DKIM)

Resend → Domains → add your sending domain → it gives you DNS records. Add them at your registrar (Namecheap/Cloudflare/etc.), then hit Verify until every row is green. Until this passes, inbox providers see "unauthenticated mail" → spam. Typical records:

TypeHost / NameValue (Resend shows the exact strings)
MXsendfeedback-smtp.{region}.amazonses.com (priority 10)
TXTsendv=spf1 include:amazonses.com ~all
TXTresend._domainkeyp=MIGfMA0… (DKIM public key)

Copy the values exactly from your Resend dashboard — the keys above are illustrative. Allow up to a few hours for DNS to propagate.

Step 3

Publish a DMARC record

This is the record most people forget — and modern Gmail bulk rules spam-folder senders without it. Add one TXT record at your registrar:

TypeHost / NameValue
TXT_dmarcv=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@findersxkeepers.com;

p=none is enough to pass — it just declares that you publish a policy. Combined with aligned SPF + DKIM from Step 2, this is the trifecta inbox providers check.

The part everyone gets confused by

Why send from a subdomain?

Think of your domain as a family with a shared last name. Every address inherits the family's reputation:

findersxkeepers.com— your website + your personal email (you@findersxkeepers.com)
└─mail.findersxkeepers.com— a dedicated room just for automated mail (OTP, receipts)

A subdomain (mail. in front) is a separate identity that inherits ownership of the root but carries its own sending reputation. You verify mail.findersxkeepers.com in Resend and send OTP from noreply@mail.findersxkeepers.com.

✓ With a sending subdomain

If automated mail ever gets a spam complaint or a bad run, only the mail. reputation takes the hit. Your root domain — your website's trust and any human email you send — stays clean.

✕ Sending from the root

Automated bulk mail and your personal/business email share one reputation. A deliverability dip on transactional mail can drag down everything on findersxkeepers.com.

Note: Resend already creates a send. subdomain automatically for the bounce/Return-Path (that's the MX row in Step 2). Using mail. for the visible From address is the extra, recommended layer. It's optional — sending from noreply@findersxkeepers.com (root) still works once Steps 2–3 pass — but the subdomain is the cleaner long-term setup.

Confirm it worked

The 30-second inbox test.

Trigger a login code to a Gmail address. Open it → ⋮ → Show original. You want all three green:

# Gmail → Show original
SPF:    PASS   with domain mail.findersxkeepers.com
DKIM:   PASS   with domain mail.findersxkeepers.com
DMARC:  PASS

All three PASS = you're authenticated and will land in the inbox. For a brand-new domain, also have a few early users hit "Not spam" + reply once — that warms reputation within a day or two. Avoid image-only emails, link shorteners, and spammy words to keep it there.
